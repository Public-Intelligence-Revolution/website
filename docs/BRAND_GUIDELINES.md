# Brand Guidelines

## Purpose

Design at Public Intelligence is not a layer of persuasion placed on top of engineering. It is the public face of a long-term civic undertaking. Its role is to make serious technical work legible, trustworthy, and calm. Good design here reduces noise, reveals structure, and helps readers evaluate substance without distraction. The website, documentation, and every public artifact should feel like part of a permanent public utility—built to be used, audited, and extended for decades.

## Emotional Goals

Public Intelligence should greet readers with quiet ambition: the sense that they have encountered the early foundations of a durable public institution, not a startup seeking attention.

- **Within 5 seconds:** The reader should feel they have stepped onto serious ground. The absence of color, motion, or sales language creates immediate respect. Seeing the project's name beside its core mission produces a subtle jolt—this is a proposition about shared technological power, not a product. The dominant feeling is calm gravity.
- **After 30 seconds:** The reader should feel intellectual trust. The copy is precise and hype-free. They understand the project is decentralized, open, and documentation-driven. If they are a builder, they feel alignment. The monochrome calm reads as confidence. They sense disciplined minds behind the words.
- **After reading the entire website:** The reader should feel they have been handed a blueprint for a decades-long public asset. They feel sober optimism—realism about difficulty paired with clarity of intent. No false urgency. They leave with steadiness: this is built to outlast trends, and they know where to look next.

Quiet ambition means we aim for civilization-scale impact while speaking in a measured, restrained voice.

## Brand Personality

Public Intelligence is:

- Thoughtful
- Precise
- Engineering-first
- Calm
- Durable
- Transparent
- Ambitious without being loud

Public Intelligence is NOT:

- Hype
- Crypto-speculative
- Futuristic gimmickry
- Startup marketing
- Artificial urgency
- AI buzzword-driven

The personality is that of a public works engineer who writes clear specs, not a founder who pitches.

## Writing Style

Every page should be written as documentation for a serious technical audience.

- **Sentence length:** Prefer short, direct sentences. Break complex thoughts into separate lines.
- **Tone:** Calm, neutral, active where practical. No exclamation. No persuasion.
- **Technical depth:** Assume the reader is technically literate. Do not flatten nuance.
- **Jargon:** Use precise technical terms when they carry meaning. Do not use vague business or AI-industry slang.
- **Marketing language:** Prohibited. No testimonials, no "seamless", no "revolutionary", no urgency cues.

## Visual Principles

- **Typography:** The primary design material. Clear, restrained, readable. Hierarchy from size and spacing, not decoration.
- **Whitespace:** Generous. Used to create scanability and rhythm. Not to simulate importance.
- **Color philosophy:** Dark-only, monochrome. One restrained accent for necessary emphasis. No gradients, no new brand colors.
- **Iconography:** Only when it communicates necessary UI function. No decorative icons.
- **Diagrams:** Allowed when they explain structure. They should be precise, monochrome, and quiet.
- **Illustrations:** None. The work is serious; imagery distracts.
- **Animation:** None unless it clarifies function. No motion for persuasion.
- **Imagery:** None. The project is explained through text and structure.

## Homepage Philosophy

The homepage is an entry point, not a campaign. Its narrative journey is deliberate:

Mission → Architecture → Progress → Contribution

The reader first meets the core intent (what we are building and why). They then see the technical shape of the system (how it is structured). Next they view current progress and roadmap (what is real today). Finally they are shown how to take part (contribution).

This order exists because understanding must precede action. A reader who knows the mission can judge the architecture. A reader who sees the architecture can trust the progress. A reader who trusts the progress will contribute meaningfully. The homepage is a guided intellectual path, not a funnel.

## Public Intelligence as an Institution

Public Intelligence is building public digital infrastructure, not a startup or a product.

If the project exists for 30 years, its name should be associated with:

- Reliable, community-owned technical commons
- Transparent engineering practiced at scale
- A stable reference point for open AI infrastructure

Every repository—whether website, documentation, scheduler, node software, research paper, or presentation—should feel like a component of the same public institution. It should feel considered, permanent, and auditable. The feeling should resemble enduring scientific and archival organizations that exist to serve the public over generations, not entities optimized for exit or growth metrics.

The values every repository communicates:

- Stewardship of shared resources
- Openness by default
- Resistance to fashion
- Long-term maintainability

Over time, contributors and users should develop a specific trust: that Public Intelligence will still be operating on the same principles in a decade, that its artifacts will be comprehensible, and that no hidden commercial shift will redirect the mission. This trust is earned through consistency, not claims.

## Long-term Consistency

Future contributors and AI agents should evaluate every design change against this constitution:

1. Does it increase calm and clarity, or add noise?
2. Does it preserve the monochrome, typography-first language?
3. Does it communicate substance before persuasion?
4. Would this change still make sense in ten years?
5. Does it respect the reader's intelligence?

If a proposed change fails these tests, it does not belong in Public Intelligence, regardless of trend. The brand is a commitment across time.
